# Fact-Check: Langworthy's DHS Shutdown Claim During Operation Epic Fury
**February 28, 2026**

### Statement
**Source:** Official Statement on Operation Epic Fury, February 28, 2026; Facebook post (same day)

In both his formal statement on Operation Epic Fury and a separate Facebook post, Rep. Langworthy pivoted from the Iran conflict to attack Democrats over DHS funding:

**From the official statement:**
> "It is also a time that we must be extra vigilant on our own soil and Democrats must stop blocking funding for the Department of Homeland Security so it can immediately reopen and be fully operational. Time is of the essence."

**From the Facebook post:**
> "Democrats are putting our country at grave risk by blocking the Department of Homeland Security from reopening. They are the agency charged with protecting our nation from terrorism and cyber attacks on our institutions. We cannot afford to have this vital agency shuttered *ever* let alone during this consequential moment. Stop the games and reopen DHS NOW!"

---

## Background: Why DHS Funding Lapsed

DHS entered a funding lapse on **February 14, 2026** — making this the third shutdown affecting the department in FY2026 alone. All 11 other federal appropriations bills had been signed into law. DHS stood alone.

The proximate cause: Senate Democrats filibustered the DHS funding bill, blocking it from reaching the 60-vote threshold needed for advancement:

- **February 12, 2026:** Cloture vote failed **52–47** (needed 60). Only Sen. John Fetterman (D-PA) voted with Republicans.
- **February 24, 2026:** Cloture vote failed **50–45** (needed 60). Again only Fetterman crossed over.

Sen. Katie Britt (R-AL) sought unanimous consent for a two-week stopgap extension, which Sen. Chris Murphy (D-CT) objected to on behalf of Democrats.

### What Langworthy Does Not Mention: Why Democrats Blocked Funding

On **January 7, 2026**, an ICE agent fatally shot **Renée Good**, a U.S. citizen and mother of three, during "Operation Metro Surge" in Minneapolis.

On **January 24, 2026**, CBP agents fatally shot **Alex Pretti**, a 37-year-old VA ICU nurse and U.S. citizen. Video evidence contradicted DHS Secretary Noem's initial characterization of the incident.

These killings prompted **bipartisan** alarm. Republican Senators Cruz, Cassidy, Collins, Murkowski, Tillis, Curtis, and Ricketts all called for investigations.

Democrats issued **10 specific reform demands** as conditions for funding: mandatory body cameras, judicial warrants for home entries, agents must identify themselves and stop wearing masks, independent use-of-force investigations, restrictions on roving patrols, and protections for sensitive locations like schools and churches.

Senate Minority Leader Schumer stated: "We will not support an extension of the status quo, a status quo that permits masked secret police to barge into people's homes without warrants."

## Claim 1: "DHS is shuttered"

**Rating: MISLEADING**

DHS experienced a real funding lapse — but "shuttered" substantially overstates the situation. **Approximately 90% of DHS's 260,000+ employees continued working** through the shutdown, many without pay. The operational picture by component:

| Component | Workforce Status | Notes |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| **ICE & CBP** | Largely unaffected | Received tens of billions in separate funding through the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) reconciliation package. CBP used OBBBA funds to pay 57,600+ employees. |
| **TSA** | ~95% of 61,000 employees working | Screeners faced partial paychecks on Feb 28; first full missed paycheck due March 14 |
| **Secret Service** | 94% on duty | Essential operations continued |
| **Coast Guard** | Most of ~56,000 on duty | Essential missions maintained |
| **FEMA** | ~84% retained | Suspended non-disaster operations; Disaster Relief Fund covered emergency response |
| **CISA** | Only ~900 of ~2,200 working (~41%) | Cybersecurity assessments, training, and stakeholder engagement all suspended |

The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget estimated roughly two-thirds of DHS's overall budget was covered by OBBBA funding. Sen. Fetterman (D-PA) noted: "This shutdown literally has zero impact on ICE functionality."

**Bottom line:** The funding lapse was real and had real consequences for unpaid workers and some components — but the agency was far from "shuttered." Its largest enforcement components operated with full funding.

---

## Claim 2: "Democrats are blocking DHS from reopening"

**Rating: MOSTLY TRUE procedurally / MISLEADING in context**

This is the most procedurally defensible part of Langworthy's statement — and the most deceptively framed. Democrats did use the Senate filibuster to block DHS funding on two recorded votes.

**What makes it misleading:**

1. **The reason is omitted entirely.** Federal agents killed two U.S. citizens in the weeks before the filibuster. Democrats demanded specific accountability reforms. Langworthy's framing presents their action as arbitrary obstruction.

2. **Republicans were not unified.** On a January 29 Senate vote on a combined six-bill package, **eight Republican senators voted no** (Johnson, Tuberville, Lee, Budd, Paul, Moody, Scott, and Thune). In the House, Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY) voted against the DHS bill.

3. **Some Democrats voted for DHS funding.** Seven House Democrats voted for the DHS funding bill on January 22. Twenty-one Democrats helped pass the February 3 two-week continuing resolution.

4. **The White House was slow to negotiate.** A White House counterproposal did not arrive until February 27 — nearly two weeks into the shutdown.

**Bottom line:** Democrats did block the bill. But framing it as "games" while omitting the precipitating cause (federal agents killing citizens), the reform demands, bipartisan concern, Republican defections, and the administration's slow response is a significant distortion.

## Claim 3: DHS is "the agency charged with protecting our nation from terrorism and cyber attacks"

**Rating: MISLEADING — overstates DHS's role with the definite article "the"**

U.S. counterterrorism and cybersecurity involve a multi-agency architecture. DHS is one important player among several:

**On counterterrorism:**
- The **FBI** is the lead federal law enforcement agency for investigating and preventing terrorism (28 C.F.R. §0.85(l)) — counterterrorism has been the FBI's top priority since 2002
- The **National Counterterrorism Center (NCTC)**, under the Director of National Intelligence, serves as the government's central knowledge bank on terrorists
- The **CIA's Counterterrorism Center** handles foreign intelligence collection
- The **Department of Defense** conducts military counterterrorism through Special Operations Command
- A GAO report (GAO-25-107030) notes that DHS and DOJ are "two agencies with statutory missions to combat domestic terrorism" — not DHS alone

**On cybersecurity:**
- **CISA** (a DHS component) is designated "the nation's cyber defense agency" — this part has more merit
- But the **NSA** and **U.S. Cyber Command** handle military and signals intelligence cyber operations
- The **FBI** investigates cyber crimes
- The **Office of the National Cyber Director** coordinates White House cyber policy

**The irony undermining Langworthy's urgency:** If he is genuinely concerned about DHS's cybersecurity capacity, the most severe threat came from the administration's own decisions — not the shutdown. **CISA lost approximately one-third of its workforce** (~1,000 of ~3,400 employees) through DOGE-driven layoffs, buyouts, reassignments, and contract cancellations throughout 2025, well before the February 2026 funding lapse. The election security program was completely eliminated. Cyber defense training was cut by $45 million. The proposed FY2026 budget would slash CISA by nearly $500 million. As of late February 2026, CISA was reportedly operating at just **38% of optimal staffing** — a crisis created primarily by executive branch decisions.

**Bottom line:** DHS plays an important role, but is not "the" agency for these missions. And the most significant degradation of DHS's cybersecurity capacity resulted from administration policies Langworthy has not criticized.

---

## Claim 4: Democrats' actions put the country "at grave risk" during "this consequential moment"

**Rating: PARTIALLY TRUE with significant context missing**

There are real risks from the DHS funding lapse — particularly for CISA's cybersecurity mission and for unpaid TSA screeners and Coast Guard families. Linking the shutdown to a shooting war with Iran is not unreasonable on its face.

However:
- The enforcement agencies most relevant to the Iran conflict (CBP, ICE, Secret Service) were largely unaffected due to separate OBBBA funding
- CISA — the component most relevant to Langworthy's "cyber attacks" argument — was already gutted to 38% staffing by the administration's own workforce cuts before the shutdown began
- NPR reported that ICE and CBP told lawmakers they wouldn't see "significant impact" on enforcement during the shutdown

**Bottom line:** Real risks exist, but the most critical operations continued. Invoking a war to pressure Democrats while omitting that the administration's own policies caused the deeper damage to the cybersecurity capabilities he cites is a significant omission.

## Who Is Actually Responsible?

Langworthy assigns sole blame to Democrats. Non-partisan sources paint a more complex picture:

**Arguments that Democrats bear responsibility:**
- Democrats used the filibuster to block funding — twice
- A Harvard-Harris poll found 53% of voters opposed Democrats' strategy, including 59% of independents

**Arguments that responsibility is shared:**
- Republicans control the presidency and both chambers of Congress
- The administration's own enforcement actions precipitated the crisis (killing two U.S. citizens)
- Multiple Republican senators voted against funding packages
- The White House was slow to make counteroffers
- An NBC News poll showed Trump's border security approval dropped to 40% approve / 60% disapprove
- During the earlier 43-day full government shutdown (Oct-Nov 2025), Navigator Research found 48% blamed Trump/Republicans vs. 34% Democrats

Former FEMA Administrator Craig Fugate, who served under both Republican Governor Jeb Bush and President Obama: "This isn't new — both parties own the blame."

---

## The Rhetorical Move: Using War to Pressure on a Domestic Fight

Langworthy's statement is structured to leverage the Iran conflict as pressure on the DHS funding fight. The pivot from "Iran is bombing civilians" to "Democrats must stop blocking DHS" creates an implied chain: Iran attacks → homeland at risk → Democrats endangering homeland.

This framing collapses several distinct issues (a foreign military conflict, a domestic budget dispute over police accountability, and cybersecurity readiness) into a single partisan attack. It omits that:
- The DHS shutdown predates Operation Epic Fury by two weeks
- The agencies most relevant to wartime homeland defense continued operating
- Democrats' demands were prompted by specific incidents of lethal force against American citizens
- The administration itself has done more to degrade CISA's capacity than the shutdown has
