# FB post: data extraction / open internet / why privacy matters
Companion to the data_extraction_card.png and the KIDS Act fact-check.

---

Keeping kids safe online is something almost everyone wants. But it is worth understanding how "age verification" actually works before we call a bill a privacy win.

Here is the part the word "verification" hides:

→ A website cannot confirm your age by asking. It routes you to a private vendor that either scans a government ID you upload or runs a biometric face scan to estimate your age.
→ So proving you are an adult means handing a company your driver's license image or a scan of your face.
→ Once collected, that data sits in commercial systems under company terms, not the stronger protections that cover public records. As the libertarian Cato Institute puts it, age checks create "a trove of attractive data for hackers."

This is what privacy researchers call data extraction, and it is why groups across the spectrum, from the libertarian Cato Institute to the EFF and the ACLU, warn that broad age mandates create privacy risk rather than privacy protection.

What is really at stake is the open internet: the ability to read, search, and speak without first proving who you are. That matters for domestic-violence survivors, whistleblowers, patients researching a diagnosis, and anyone who simply values their privacy.

The NY-23 record:

→ Rep. Langworthy voted YEA on the KIDS Act (Roll Call 228), which pushes age checks, and called it a bill that "strengthens privacy protections for children."
→ He also cosponsors the SECURE Data Act (H.R. 8413), which contains no private right of action, meaning you could not sue a company that misuses your data, and which would preempt stronger state privacy laws.

Privacy is not about having something to hide. It is about who controls your data once you hand it over.
