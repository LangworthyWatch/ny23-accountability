One committee decides what the entire House gets to vote on. It writes the terms for nearly every major bill, including which amendments, if any, reach the floor. Nick Langworthy holds New York's only seat on it. Of the state's 26 House members, he is the one on the Rules Committee.

Here is what his own recorded, by-name committee votes show he does with that seat, across four unrelated bills:

→ MEDICAID (H.R. 1): voted to keep off the floor the amendments to strike the cuts, make the ACA premium credits permanent, and cap insulin at $35
→ TARIFFS: twice voted to keep the procedural "off switch" that blocked the House's guaranteed vote to end the tariffs hitting his own district's manufacturers
→ VETERANS (H.R. 9237): voted to keep off the floor the amendment that would have passed the Major Richard Star Act without the disability-benefit cuts
→ EPSTEIN: voted against forcing up the Epstein Files Transparency Act, and against holding the Attorney General in contempt for withholding the files

The subjects have nothing in common. The vote does. Every time: NO on letting the House vote on the popular fix, YES on the rule that closed the door.

To be fair, these are procedural votes, and closed rules are routine majority behavior. But he markets this exact seat as the district's "seat at the table on major legislation." The record of how he uses it is uniform.
