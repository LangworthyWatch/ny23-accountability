Prosecuting fraud in taxpayer-funded programs is something everyone should support — and in Minnesota, it happened. The question is who did it.

Rep. Langworthy posted this week that "without our House Oversight Committee investigation, this scandal would have stayed buried." Here's the documented timeline:

→ January 2022: the FBI executed search warrants at 26 locations across Minnesota in early-morning raids
→ September 2022: the DOJ charged 47 people in the $250 million scheme — the U.S. Attorney announced it at a national press conference
→ Since then: 77+ people indicted, more than 50 convicted, and the ringleader sentenced to 500 months — over 41 years in federal prison
→ January 2026: the Oversight Committee held its FIRST hearing — four years after the raids

The committee did add something real: its report on what Minnesota officials knew led to this week's DOJ referral of Gov. Walz and AG Ellison. That's its genuine contribution. (A referral is a request to investigate — not a charge. Career prosecutors decide what comes next.) But examining an already-prosecuted scandal isn't the same as exposing one.

He also called it "a 50 state, coast to coast effort." The public record shows committee investigations in three states: Minnesota, New York, and California.

And in those states, enforcement was already underway:

→ New York: this same week, AG James joined a bipartisan coalition of 36 other attorneys general and the DOJ to secure $36.5 million from CVS for overbilling Medicaid for insulin from 2010 to 2020
→ California: in April, state agencies brought criminal charges dismantling an LA hospice fraud scheme — 14 fraudulent providers and more than $267 million in improper Medi-Cal claims

States catching Medicaid fraud isn't a hypothetical. It's the existing record.
