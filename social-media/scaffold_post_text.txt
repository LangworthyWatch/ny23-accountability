Rep. Langworthy says New York's Scaffold Law is "outdated" and that it drives up housing costs, infrastructure costs, and taxes. Wanting to make New York more affordable is fair enough.

Here is what the posts leave out:

→ The Scaffold Law (NY Labor Law §240, in effect since 1885) is a worker fall-protection law. It makes property owners and contractors liable when a construction worker is injured in a fall.

→ Falls are the leading cause of construction-worker deaths. In 2023, 74 New York construction workers died on the job, the highest in a decade (NYCOSH).

→ The cost figures he amplifies ("$785 million a year," insurance "200% to 500% higher") come from the construction employers' association that would benefit from repeal, not from independent analysis. Rep. Adriano Espaillat (D-NY) calls the 10% cost claim "advocacy math, not credible economics."

→ Construction unions oppose it. NYS AFL-CIO President Mario Cilento says the bill would "invalidate vital safety protections for construction workers," and called it "beyond comprehension that the congressman is going to Washington to undermine workers in his own state."

→ The bill (H.R. 3548) has 5 Republican cosponsors and is stuck in committee, so an industry coalition is now trying to attach it to the must-pass federal highway bill before the current law expires in September 2026.

You can want lower construction costs and still want to know that the law on the table is the one that protects workers who fall. His post makes the cost case in full and never mentions the safety one.
