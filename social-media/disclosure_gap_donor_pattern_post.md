## Facebook

Campaign donations are public. Votes are public. Financial disclosures are public. What's missing is the part that connects them, and no one is required to say it out loud.

We pulled the public records on Rep. Langworthy's announcements and lined up the donors with a stake in each one. A pattern shows up:

→ The Seneca Nation gave $10,100. Then he introduced a bill to strip New York's jurisdiction over Seneca lands. The press release never mentioned the donations.
→ A nursing-home operator gave $68,700, every dollar before the vote. Then he voted to block the first federal nursing-home staffing standard for 10 years.
→ 62 Corning employees gave $65,775 around a reconciliation bill that preserved the manufacturing tax credits the company relies on.
→ The national homebuilders' PAC gave $16,500. Then he publicized their "Defender of Housing" award, which is handed out for backing the group's positions.

None of this is illegal. Campaign contributions are legal and routine, and we are not claiming any donation bought a vote. The point is narrower: when he announces what he has done, the donor relationship already sitting in the public record is the part he leaves out.
