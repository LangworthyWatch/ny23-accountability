## Why This Matters

Rep. Langworthy posted that "some groups are misleading veterans" on the Take Care of America's Veterans Act (H.R. 9237) and "would rather focus on spreading fear through dangerous graphics than speak about the historic policies in the legislation." The post is partly right: the bill contains real benefit expansions, and one specific fear — that current veterans will lose their existing ratings — is not what the bill does. But the framing gets the central fact backward. The groups raising the alarm are not fringe actors "spreading fear"; they are the largest veterans service organizations in the country — the VFW, DAV, and IAVA — and the provision they are warning about is written into the bill. This entry documents both what the bill does and who is actually objecting.

---

## Statement

**Source:** Facebook Post, June 2026

> "It's clear some groups are misleading veterans on the Take Care of America's Act. They would rather focus on spreading fear through dangerous graphics than speak about the historic policies in the legislation. HR 9237 delivers MAJOR benefit expansions – including caregiver reforms, support for student veterans, and more support for catastrophically disabled veterans."

*(The bill's full title is the "Take Care of America's **Veterans** Act.")*

---

## What He Gets Right

Two things in the post are accurate, and a fair reading has to start there.

**The benefit expansions are real.** H.R. 9237 (sponsored by House Veterans' Affairs Committee Chairman Mike Bost, introduced June 10, 2026, consolidating more than 60 bills) does contain the expansions he names:

- **Caregiver reforms** — a new bowel-and-bladder care program (Sec. 332) and caregiver reemployment/retirement assistance plus 180 days of post-discharge coverage (Sec. 302).
- **Student-veteran support** — Title II, including a Post-9/11 housing stipend for distance-learning summer terms (Sec. 203).
- **Catastrophically disabled veterans** — a new supplemental aid-and-attendance allowance (Sec. 104).

The bill's centerpiece is the **Major Richard Star Act** (Sec. 101), a popular bipartisan measure letting certain combat-disabled retirees receive both retirement pay and disability compensation. Support for that is genuine and broad.

**And the strongest "fear" would be inaccurate.** The disputed cut applies **only to new claims filed after enactment. Sec. 108(c)(2) explicitly protects existing compensation** — no current veteran's rating is reduced. If a graphic told current recipients they would lose their benefits, that graphic would be wrong, and Langworthy would be right to say so.

---

## What the Post Leaves Out

**The bill does cut future disability compensation** — for two of the most common service-connected conditions, as the pay-for for the Star Act. Section 108 rewrites the rating rules so that, for claims filed after enactment:

- **Sleep apnea** is rated **0%** when controlled or asymptomatic (i.e., managed with a CPAP machine), and reaches higher ratings only when treatment is ineffective or there is end-organ damage — effectively ending compensation for the CPAP-controlled apnea that is common today.
- **Tinnitus** no longer carries a separate compensable rating except a 10% rating tied to otherwise-noncompensable hearing loss.

Roughly **1.3 million veterans currently receive compensation for sleep apnea and about 1.5 million for tinnitus**. According to a **VA analysis cited by the DAV, VFW, and IAVA**, the rating changes would reduce future disability payments by about **$57 billion over ten years** and affect **up to 1.5 million veterans** over time. (No Congressional Budget Office score was located; this figure is VA-analysis-sourced via the veterans groups, and is attributed as such.)

The bill also **raises VA home-loan funding fees** (the refinancing fee rises from 0.50% to 1.42%, the loan-assumption fee from 0.50% to 1.0%; Sec. amending 38 U.S.C. §3729), **reclassifies roughly 5,000 VA psychologists** into a different personnel system (Sec. 631), and **expands private-sector Community Care** (Title VI) — provisions unions and some VSOs describe as eroding VA care and workers' rights.

## Who Is Actually Warning Veterans

The post's core claim is that "some groups" are "misleading veterans" with "dangerous graphics." The groups on record opposing the bill are the mainstream veterans service organizations:

- **VFW** (the nation's largest combat-veterans organization) — National Commander Carol Whitmore: *"A grateful nation pays its debts to veterans; it does not send them the invoice."* The VFW called the rating changes "a dangerous precedent by allowing Congress to alter disability ratings for budgetary purposes."
- **DAV** — "DAV condemns congressional proposal to cut disability benefits for 1.5 million veterans" (June 10, 2026); National Commander Coleman Nee called it a "budget-driven decision that shifts the burden onto those who have already sacrificed … This is wrong and it must be stopped."
- **IAVA** — opposes the bill and its "poison pill pay-fors."
- **AFGE** (the largest VA employees' union) and the **AFL-CIO** also oppose it.

These are not fringe organizations, and their central factual claim — that the bill reduces future disability compensation to offset the Star Act — is in the text. In fairness, the bill also has support: the **American Legion**, the **Elizabeth Dole Foundation**, and **Americans for Prosperity** back it, largely for the caregiver, survivor, and Star Act provisions.

**The clearest tell:** as opposition mounted, **House leadership pulled H.R. 9237 from the floor** around June 25, 2026, and it has not received a House passage vote. Leadership does not shelve its own chairman's bill over "dangerous graphics."

---

## His Own Role in the Record

Langworthy is **not a cosponsor** of H.R. 9237 (the 30 cosponsors include New York's Lawler, Malliotakis, and Tenney, but not him). His documented action on the bill came on the **Rules Committee**, where he sits. At the June 23, 2026 meeting (H. Rept. 119-707), by recorded vote:

- He voted **Nay (record vote 369)** on a motion to make in order **Rep. Takano's amendment** — which would have **passed the Major Richard Star Act on its own, using Defense funds from the 2025 reconciliation law as the offset instead of the disability-rating cuts.** In other words, an option to deliver the popular centerpiece *without* the cut the VSOs oppose. It was defeated 4–8.
- He voted **Yea (record vote 373)** to report the **closed rule** (H.Res.1377), which barred all amendments to the bill on the floor.

He then posted publicly that the objectors were "misleading veterans." He had a recorded chance, in committee, to advance the Star Act without the disputed cut, and voted to keep that option off the floor.

---

## What Is — and Isn't — Established

**Documented from the bill text and official records:** the real expansions (Secs. 104, 302, 332, Title II); the Star Act centerpiece (Sec. 101); the future-claims-only disability-rating cuts and the existing-ratings protection (Sec. 108, incl. 108(c)(2)); the home-loan fee increases (§3729), psychologist reclassification (Sec. 631), and Community Care expansion (Title VI); the VFW/DAV/IAVA/AFGE/AFL-CIO opposition and the American Legion/Dole Foundation/AFP support; the pulled floor vote; and Langworthy's recorded Rules votes (H. Rept. 119-707, votes 369 and 373).

**Attributed, not asserted as fact:** the "$57 billion / up to 1.5 million veterans" figure, which comes from a VA analysis cited by the veterans groups, not a CBO score.

**Not claimed:** that current veterans lose their ratings (they do not, per Sec. 108(c)(2)); that every opposing graphic is accurate; or that Langworthy sponsored the bill (he did not).

---

## Questions This Raises

1. If the objections are "misleading," why do they come from the VFW, DAV, and IAVA — and why did House leadership pull the bill rather than hold the vote?
2. Section 108 reduces future disability compensation for sleep apnea and tinnitus to help pay for the Star Act. Is describing that trade-off "spreading fear," or accurately reading the bill?
3. On the Rules Committee, why vote to keep off the floor the amendment that would have delivered the Major Richard Star Act *without* the disability-rating cuts?
