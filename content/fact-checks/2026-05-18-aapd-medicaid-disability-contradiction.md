## Why This Matters for NY-23

Medicaid is the single largest source of health coverage in NY-23 — a predominantly rural district with above-average rates of disability, poverty, and older residents. For people with disabilities, Medicaid funds home and community-based services, personal care aides, supported employment, and long-term care. These services have no private-market substitute. When Medicaid is cut, that care shifts to families or disappears.

## Congressional Record

**H.R. 1 — One Big Beautiful Bill Act, enacted July 4, 2025**
Langworthy voted **YEA** (Roll Call 190, July 3, 2025). He gave floor remarks in support and released a statement calling passage "a generational win for the American people."

The Congressional Budget Office estimated the Medicaid-related provisions of H.R. 1 would reduce federal Medicaid spending by **$840 billion over ten years** (FY2025–FY2034). Total health coverage reductions, including CHIP, reach **$907.5 billion**. CBO projects the number of uninsured Americans increases by 10 million by 2034.

Key Medicaid provisions in the enacted law:
- Work reporting requirements for non-elderly adults (administrative burden historically causes eligible enrollees — including people with disabilities — to lose coverage)
- Changes to federal matching rates (FMAP) affecting state program capacity
- Reductions to home and community-based services funding

## The AAPD's Response to the Same Law

The American Association of People with Disabilities — the organization whose representatives Langworthy met on May 18 — responded to the passage of H.R. 1 with a statement from President and CEO Maria Town:

> **"This Is A Devastating Day for Disabled Americans."**

The AAPD's Medicaid Defense Hub describes the law as "a cruel assault on disabled people." The organization has opposed the legislation throughout the legislative process.

## Assessment

**Verdict: CONTRADICTION**

Langworthy voted for H.R. 1 (enacted July 4, 2025), which the Congressional Budget Office estimates will cut Medicaid by $840 billion over ten years — reductions that directly affect the healthcare access, home-based services, and long-term support programs the American Association of People with Disabilities exists to protect. Ten months after that vote, he met with AAPD representatives and pledged to "keep working to protect critical support systems." His recorded vote is the direct evidence of what he did to those systems.
