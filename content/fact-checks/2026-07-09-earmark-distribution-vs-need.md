## Why this matters

Rep. Langworthy regularly announces federal dollars "delivered" for NY-23. Most
of that money is **formula or competitive grants** decided by federal agencies —
he does not choose the recipients (see our companion entries on the earmark-vs-
grant distinction). The one pot he *does* control is **Community Project Funding
(CPF)** — member-directed earmarks he personally selects. Because those are his
choices, their distribution is a fair measure of his priorities. This entry maps
where his earmark dollars went, against where the need is.

## The Facts

Two appropriations laws actually funded his earmarks: **FY2024** (P.L. 118-42)
and **FY2026** (P.L. 119-74). **FY2025 funded none** — Congress passed a
full-year continuing resolution that carried no Community Project Funding, so the
projects he requested for FY2025 were re-submitted and funded (if at all) in
FY2026. Across the two enacted cycles, his office accounts for roughly
**$31 million** in in-district CPF (verified against the enacted FY2024
subcommittee CPF tables and his FY2026 releases; a further ~$2.7M of his earmarks
are for neighboring NY-26 — see "Outside his district" below). Set against U.S.
Census ACS 2024 county data (poverty rate, population), the per-resident
distribution is:

| County | Poverty rate | CPF projects | CPF $ | $ per resident |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Erie (NY-23 portion) | 13.9% | 4 | $13.2M | $85.16 |
| Allegany | 16.2% | 4 | $3.93M | $83.34 |
| Cattaraugus | 19.1% | 7 | $6.22M | $81.62 |
| Schuyler | 14.8% | 1 | $1.00M | $56.94 |
| Chautauqua | 17.4% | 4 | $4.35M | $34.65 |
| Steuben | 13.8% | 2 | $2.23M | $24.13 |
| **Chemung** | 16.3% | 1 | $0.48M | **$5.84** |
| Tioga | 13.4% | 0 | $0 | $0.00 |

*How these are counted: every project above is a member-directed **Community
Project Funding** item that appears on Langworthy's official CPF /
appropriations-request disclosures. Water and sewer earmarks are obligated
through EPA's State Revolving Fund / STAG account, and his office has announced
some of them as "EPA grants" — it is the same directed money. Formula and
competitive grants he announced but did not direct (FAA, FEMA, ARC, USDA Rural
Development) are excluded. Figures were verified against the **enacted FY2024
subcommittee CPF tables** (House Appropriations), which list each project with
"Langworthy" as requester — across Interior-Environment (EPA water/sewer),
Commerce-Justice-Science (St. Bonaventure, Chautauqua Sheriff), and
Transportation-HUD (FeedMore, Seneca/Salamanca, Allegany fairgrounds) — plus his
FY2026 releases. Two Army Corps of Engineers projects he requested (Barcelona
Harbor, Chautauqua Lake) are excluded as federal Corps works, not municipal
grants. Portville is $1,250,000 (his release rounds it to "over $1.2M").*

Two findings:

1. **The dollars track need — with one exception.** Earmark dollars-per-resident
   show a positive correlation with county poverty (Pearson **r = +0.33**). The
   three best-served counties per resident are his home Erie ($85) and the
   district's **two poorest** — Allegany (16.2%, $83) and Cattaraugus (19.1%,
   $82) — a near three-way tie. The distribution is need-oriented, not need-blind.
   The one exception is Chemung (below).

2. **The home-county tilt is marginal.** His two largest single earmarks — a
   **$5M Newstead community center** (a joint request with Sens. Schumer and
   Gillibrand) and **$4.2M for Erie County Sheriff's Office helicopters** — are in
   his home Erie County. But Erie holds only 42% of the dollars, and once
   out-of-district items are removed its per-resident figure ($85) essentially
   ties the two poorest counties. The sheriff's helicopters are his single biggest
   *solo* earmark; Newstead is shared with the senators (see below).

**In plain language:** His earmark dollars broadly favor poorer rural counties —
the two poorest, Cattaraugus and Allegany, do about as well per person as his own
home county. The real stories are narrower: one populous, higher-poverty county
(Chemung) is left almost entirely out; his biggest projects were secured first by
the senators he attacks; and some of what he claims isn't even in his district.

## The Chemung gap

The clearest outlier is **Chemung County** — the district's 2nd-most-populous
county and 3rd-highest in poverty (16.3%), yet lowest in earmark dollars per
resident ($5.84) among counties that received any — only Tioga, the district's
lowest-poverty county, got none. Chemung sits **entirely within
NY-23** — Langworthy is its only member of the House, and Elmira is one of the
district's two largest cities — so this is not a split-representation artifact.
Yet across all three request cycles, Langworthy's *only* Chemung CPF selection
was a **$480,000 award to Elmira College**, a private institution — not a
municipal water, sewer, or public-safety project of the kind he directed
elsewhere. Chemung's public infrastructure needs
were instead met with smaller **formula** grants he announced but did not direct
(e.g., a $248,815 Appalachian Regional Commission grant for a Chemung County
canal connector, July 2025).

---

## Who secured it first

On his biggest *shared* projects, the two Democratic senators he routinely
attacks were there **first** — and larger. Langworthy took office in January 2023,
so his earliest possible earmark cycle was FY2024. Sens. Schumer and Gillibrand
had already been requesting and funding many of the same communities before he
could file a single request:

| Shared project | Senators (first) | Langworthy (later) |
|---|---|---|
| West Seneca water *(NY-26)* | FY2022 request: **$7,388,000** | FY2024: $1,229,360 |
| Portville water | FY2022 request: **$5,250,000** (≈$3.5M funded) | FY2024: $1,250,000 |
| Mayville / N. Chautauqua Lake Sewer | FY2022: $2,000,000 | FY2024: $2,000,000 |

Roughly **half** of Langworthy's own earmark dollars (~$17.7M) are on projects the
senators also fund; the other **half** (~$14.9M) are genuinely solo — led by the
$4.2M sheriff's helicopters. And the senators independently fund NY-23 projects he
has **no part in**, including in the counties he most neglects: a **$1M Chemung
County wastewater** project (FY2023) and **~$3.1M across Tioga County** (which he
gave $0). On the overlapping work he is the follow-on partner, not the originator.

## Outside his district

Several earmarks Langworthy requested or claimed are for his home Western New York
region **outside NY-23** — in neighboring NY-26 (Rep. Tim Kennedy) or Niagara
County:

- **Town of West Seneca — $1,229,360** (FY2024): a water district in **NY-26**.
  (The senators had requested $7.4M for it in FY2022.)
- **Buffalo Niagara International Airport — $1,482,000** (FY2026): the facility is
  in Cheektowaga, **NY-26**.
- **Wendelville Fire Company** (FY2026 request): **Niagara County**, not NY-23.
- **FeedMore WNY — $3,000,000**: a regional food bank whose facility is in
  Buffalo/Hamburg (**NY-26**), though it serves NY-23 counties.

The county table above counts only in-district projects (West Seneca and the
airport are excluded). The pattern is worth noting on its own: he directs federal
money to his home region even where he is not the representative.

## Context (the fairness caveat — must resolve before publishing)

Community Project Funding requires a **local applicant** — a municipality,
county, or eligible nonprofit must submit a project to the member's office, and a
member may forward only up to 15 per year. Chemung's low earmark total therefore
has two possible explanations that the public record does not distinguish:

- Chemung/Elmira governments **applied and were not selected**, or
- they **submitted fewer or no CPF applications** for the member to forward.

This entry does **not** assert favoritism or a deliberate snub. It documents a
distribution that does not match need and a populous, higher-poverty county left
largely out of the earmark pool. Resolving which explanation applies (via the
county/city, or the member's office) is required before any stronger framing.

## Questions This Raises

1. Why did Chemung County — 2nd-most-populous, 3rd-poorest — receive the fewest
   earmark dollars per resident, and its only earmark go to a private college?
2. Did Elmira or other Chemung municipalities submit Community Project Funding
   requests that were not forwarded?
3. Should the two largest single earmarks (a $5M community center and $4.2M in
   sheriff's helicopters) sit in the representative's home county while poorer
   counties received smaller water and sewer awards?
4. Why does he request and claim earmarks for communities **outside NY-23**
   (West Seneca and the Buffalo airport in NY-26; Wendelville Fire in Niagara
   County) — and present projects the senators secured first as "I secured"?
