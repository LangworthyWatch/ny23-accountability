## Why This Matters

Rep. Langworthy's post marks the one-year anniversary of Public Law 119-21 (the "One Big Beautiful Bill Act") and calls it "the LARGEST TAX CUT in U.S. history," listing five family perks: no tax on tips, no tax on overtime, a bigger Child Tax Credit, a higher standard deduction, and an auto-loan interest deduction. The post displays H.Res.1383 (Van Duyne, June 24, 2026), which commemorates the law.

Most of those provisions are real. The headline is not — and the parts the post leaves out are the parts that decide who the law was built for.

## The Facts

| Claim | Verdict | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| P.L. 119-21 enacted July 4, 2025 (reconciliation, H.R. 1) | **TRUE** | — |
| H.Res.1383 (Van Duyne, June 24, 2026) commemorates the law | **TRUE** | Referred to Ways and Means |
| The five "family" provisions exist | **TRUE but misleading by omission** | See below |
| **"LARGEST TAX CUT in U.S. history"** | **FALSE** | ~6th largest since 1940 as a share of GDP (Tax Foundation) |

### The five "family" provisions, in full

| Provision | Real? | Omitted caveats |
|---|---|---|
| No tax on tips | Yes — a *deduction* | Capped ~$25k; phases out above $150k/$300k; **expires after 2028** |
| No tax on overtime | Yes — a *deduction* | Capped $12.5k/$25k; same phase-outs; **expires after 2028** |
| Auto-loan interest | Yes | Capped $10k; US-assembled vehicles only; **expires after 2028** |
| Bigger Child Tax Credit | Yes | $2,000 → $2,200; **permanent**, inflation-indexed |
| Higher standard deduction | Yes | Largely extends 2017 levels; **permanent** |

The permanent business and estate provisions — 100% bonus depreciation, R&D expensing, the §199A pass-through deduction, and a $15M/$30M estate-tax exemption — outlast all three "no tax" items workers are told to celebrate.

### "Largest in history" — why it is false

Measured as a share of GDP — the standard way economists compare tax cuts across eras — the Tax Foundation ranks the law about **sixth largest since 1940**, at 1.40% of GDP. Five were larger, led by Reagan's 1981 cut at 2.89% (more than double). It is "largest" only in raw nominal dollars, a metric that misleads because both prices and the economy grow over time.

## Context: who gains, and who pays

*The figures below use the standard current-law (CBO/JCT) baseline. Where a number depends on the baseline, that is stated.*

- **It adds to the debt.** CBO scores the law at **$3.4 trillion** in added deficits over ten years — **$4.1 trillion** with interest. Most "family" provisions expire in 2028; if Congress extends them (as it did the 2017 cuts), the cost climbs to **$5.5 trillion**. The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget calls the 2028 cliff a "timing gimmick."
- **Growth doesn't pay for it.** Nonpartisan models find growth recoups only ~16–19% of the cost; CBO's dynamic score comes in *higher* (~$4.7T) because added debt raises interest rates and crowds out investment.
- **Most of it flows up.** The Penn Wharton Budget Model finds ~70% of the law's value goes to the top 10% of households (~80% by 2033). The top fifth gains ~$65,000 over a lifetime; the middle fifth gains under $500.
- **The lowest-income families help pay for it now.** The tax cuts are partly offset by ~$1.1 trillion in Medicaid/health cuts and ~$206 billion in SNAP cuts. Penn Wharton finds the bottom fifth loses income by 2030 and concludes "all future generations are worse off."

**The honest caveat.** That $3.4T uses current-law scoring (the longstanding convention). The bill's authors used a "current-policy" baseline that treats extending the 2017 cuts as free, making the law look near deficit-neutral — a ~$3.8T difference. That objection is not partisan: Republican budget hawks rejected it themselves (Rep. Chip Roy called it "fairy dust"; Budget Chairman Jodey Arrington called it "an absolute non-starter"). The borrowing is real either way.

**In plain language:** The temporary provisions go to workers and disappear after 2028; the permanent provisions go to capital and to large estates. The post advertises the first and omits the second, the debt, the upward tilt, and the health-and-food cuts that pay for part of it.

---

## Questions This Raises

1. If the "no tax on tips/overtime/auto-loan" provisions expire in 2028 while the corporate and estate-tax cuts are permanent, who was the law built to serve over the long run?
2. Why does the anniversary post omit the ~$1.1 trillion in Medicaid/health and ~$206 billion in SNAP reductions that partially fund the tax cuts?
3. On what metric is it "the largest tax cut in U.S. history," given it ranks about sixth since 1940 as a share of the economy?
