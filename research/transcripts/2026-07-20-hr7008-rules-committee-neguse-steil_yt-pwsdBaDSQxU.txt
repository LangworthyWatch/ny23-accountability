Chairman [Foxx] recognizes the gentleman from Colorado, Mr. Neguse, for his time
of questioning.

NEGUSE: Thank you. Thank you, Mr. Chairman. Thank you, Chairman Steil and Ranking
Member Morelle, for being here and for your testimony. Chairman Steil, so the name
of your bill is the Stop Insider Trading Act, correct?
STEIL: That's correct.
NEGUSE: Okay. You think that's an accurate description of the bill?
STEIL: Yeah.
NEGUSE: Okay. What does trading mean to you?
STEIL: The buying and selling for profit.
NEGUSE: The buying and selling for profit. I couldn't agree with you more. I look
at Webster's dictionary defines it as the business of buying and selling. This
bill, as you said, bans the buying of individual shares of stock, right?
STEIL: That's correct.
NEGUSE: It does not ban the selling.
STEIL: It reforms the selling, of course. It makes changes.
NEGUSE: The selling, right? You're allowed to sell as much stock as you would
like as a member of Congress under this bill. Is that wrong?
STEIL: I think it's insufficient to describe it that way, but what you said is
accurate but misleading.
NEGUSE: Yes.
STEIL: [confirms]
NEGUSE: Accurate but misleading, yes.
STEIL: Correct. Yes, it is accurate.
NEGUSE: Accurate but misleading, yes. So, let's say there's a Republican senator
who decides that they're going to sell millions of dollars of stock the week
before the COVID-19 pandemic begins in earnest. That's legal under your bill,
right?
STEIL: Of course, they would have provided a minimum of 7 days advance notice.
I'm not sure that you could do that in the timeline you just proposed.
NEGUSE: This isn't a hypothetical. A Republican senator, 7, 8, 9 days before the
COVID-19 pandemic began in earnest, sold millions of dollars of stock. Under
this bill, that would not be illegal, right?
STEIL: But of course, they would provide notice. That's the part that you don't
want to discuss.
NEGUSE: [pressing] You could defend it on the merits. You've clearly decided
that the buying of stock is a practice that you don't believe should be
permitted by members of Congress. I agree. But for some reason you've decided
that the sale of individual stocks is completely fine but for the notice
requirements you've now imposed in this bill, and I don't quite understand that
disconnect, because there are many bills — including a bill I introduced 7
years ago with Chip Roy and other members of this body that has 140
co-sponsors — that would ensure that the sale AND the purchase of stocks is
banned. And this bill does not do that. I'm just trying to understand why
you've decided that it's permissible on one end but not the other.
STEIL: [responds re: preventing profit from insider information; the
pre-disclosure requirement is meant to make any advance sale "market moving"
so the profit is eliminated once disclosed]
NEGUSE: If you're the chairman of the Intel Committee and today you come across
information that compels you to sell $500,000 worth of stock in a particular
company that you own, you make that disclosure under the Steil bill, right?
7 days notice?
STEIL: Minimum 7.
NEGUSE: [continues questioning why the same disclosure logic isn't applied to
purchases] Then why not do that on the purchase?
STEIL: [explains the bill's rationale: members entering Congress with
pre-existing holdings — e.g., shares bought before taking office, for a home
down payment or similar — should be able to divest without being forced to
hold; the goal is to stop NEW purchases while allowing existing holders to
sell with advance, market-moving notice]
NEGUSE: [closing on this point] ...140 members of this body, Republicans and
Democrats, have clearly, unambiguously said we should ban the sale or purchase
of stocks, period, full stop. There's a bill by one of the members of this
committee — I look forward to making an amendment to this bill to consider
substituting Mr. Roy's bill, which would accomplish what I've described.

[Neguse then asks about scope]
NEGUSE: Under this bill, can you purchase crypto holdings?
STEIL: It makes no changes to the House rules in that regard.
NEGUSE: So current House rules still apply — you're allowed to buy crypto.
[confirmed] Individual placements — can you do individual placement of stock
under this bill?
STEIL: Individual shares of stock, no. Private placements, yes — current House
rules would apply.
NEGUSE: Under current House rules, members of Congress can buy crypto holdings,
and under your bill that does not change, right?
STEIL: No change.
NEGUSE: Can buy stocks through private placements, and that does not change?
STEIL: No change.
NEGUSE: Can buy industry-specific mutual funds?
STEIL: That's accurate. No change.
NEGUSE: Can buy bonds?
STEIL: [confirms]

[Prediction markets]
NEGUSE: The House Administration Committee has jurisdiction over member
participation in prediction markets, right? You're the chair, you've got
principal jurisdiction.
STEIL: Yes.
NEGUSE: Four months ago, the United States Senate, on a bipartisan basis, on a
voice vote, said no senator is allowed to make bets on prediction markets —
every Republican, every Democrat agreed to it. Why can't we? We could do a
one-page resolution, cut and paste the Senate resolution, and pass it
tomorrow. Will you help us with that?
STEIL: Yes, I've been supportive of making restrictions on prediction markets...
NEGUSE: Why isn't it in this bill?
STEIL: We have two different pieces of legislation moving; prediction markets
are reasonably new and novel...
MORELLE: [interjecting] We can do it by resolution, apply it to House members
and their families/dependents, exactly what the Senate did. The alternative is
to do it in a bill, which isn't even in front of us for reasons that completely
escape my understanding. There should be a provision in this bill, as bad as
this bill is, [for] prediction markets. But yes, to your point, we could do a
resolution today.

[Voter ID / Section 3]
NEGUSE: I want to ask about the vote — the "Save Vote Act," or [asking for the
correct title] — what's this new version called? The voter ID, or Section 3
of the bill?
STEIL: [confirms it is voter ID / Section 3]
NEGUSE: My understanding is that in Wisconsin, voters approved by constitutional
amendment essentially a codification of the voter ID law already on the books.
STEIL: That's accurate.
NEGUSE: NCSL has described Wisconsin's constitutional measure — same as the
statutory measure — as the most restrictive in the country. Have you seen
that?
STEIL: I haven't seen that term, but it's a reasonably good photo ID law.
NEGUSE: Reasonably good photo ID law. Under that law, you can use a college ID.
STEIL: [confirms]
NEGUSE: And under your bill, you can't.
STEIL: Correct.
NEGUSE: I'm trying to understand why, if that "reasonably good" standard is
good enough for your own constituents in Wisconsin — many of whom comply with
what's been described as the most strict voter ID law in the country by using
a college ID — their own congressman is championing a bill that would tell
them they no longer can do that.
STEIL: Universities across the country, including in my home state, issue IDs
to non-citizens, including illegal immigrants — as do many states, including
Illinois with driver's licenses. This is a standard to protect the integrity
of elections, and we believe the best way to do that is through the forms of
ID listed in the bill.
NEGUSE: So the Wisconsin standard you called "reasonably good" five minutes ago
doesn't meet the bar here at the federal level?
STEIL: The Wisconsin standard is way better than a lot of other states — a lot
of states have nothing. Minnesota, for instance, lets you vouch for eight
people; I think that's disastrous.
NEGUSE: What's the cure period under this bill if you vote provisionally
without photo ID?
STEIL: [after some back-and-forth] 72 hours / 3 days.
NEGUSE: And if you don't cure within that window, your vote doesn't count?
STEIL: Correct, you would not have cured your ballot.
NEGUSE: Do you know what that cure period is in Wisconsin?
STEIL/MORELLE: It's a lot longer — Wisconsin also has many weeks of early
voting, so a voter could go in person, day after day, to fix it.
NEGUSE: [closing] I believe Colorado has the gold standard, but if [72 hours]
is good enough for the people of Wisconsin... [time expires]
CHAIR: Gentleman yields back.
